Abstract
Large language models are becoming useful interfaces for chemistry and materials research, but rigorous scientific use requires more than conversational ability: agents must retrieve domain knowledge, select tools, execute workflows, produce reusable artifacts, and be evaluated with objective task-native metrics. Here we present GVIM 2.0, a multi-agent research system for chemistry and materials science. In a 400-question public benchmark suite spanning ChemBench, MaScQA, and ChemLLMBench, the full GVIM workflow answered 325/400 questions correctly (81.25%), compared with 285/400 (71.25%) for a same-base API-only setting; the paired gain was 10.0 percentage points (McNemar exact p = 3.40 × 10⁻⁶; bootstrap 95% CI, 6.00–14.25 percentage points). We further evaluate five task-native evaluation workflows initiated from the GVIM front end, covering molecular solubility prediction, steel yield-strength prediction, reaction-table extraction, MS/MS spectral retrieval, and chemical entity extraction. These evaluation workflows use public gold standards and objective metrics including MAE, RMSE, R², precision, recall, F1, and top-k accuracy. Two retrospective fixed-budget discovery cases further evaluated BACE inhibitor and Matbench experimental band-gap prioritization. In an additional label-withheld temporal BACE1 evaluation, a model selected on 5,296 historical compounds was evaluated on 1,598 non-overlapping post-2018 compounds, giving moderate external regression performance (MAE = 0.822, RMSE = 1.028, R² = 0.369) and non-random prioritization (EF10 = 2.61; EF10 = 2.46 for 1,275 scaffold-novel compounds). Together, these results support GVIM 2.0 as a reproducible platform for tool-augmented chemical and materials informatics, while also identifying the additional prospective validation and competitive baselines needed before making discovery-level performance claims.

1. Introduction
For a long time, the development of chemistry and materials science has relied primarily on researchers’ empirical judgment, heuristic rules, theoretical models, computational simulations, and extensive trial-and-error experimentation. Osaro et al. [1] noted that materials discovery and chemical synthesis were historically guided by empirical knowledge, manually curated databases, phase diagrams, design charts, heuristic principles, and expert intuition. Li et al. [2] described the evolution of materials research as a progression from intuition-driven trial-and-error exploration, to theoretical models based on thermodynamics and physical laws, and subsequently to computational simulation- and artificial intelligence-assisted research paradigms. Gupta et al. [3] also pointed out that materials discovery has long depended on intuition, experimentation, and serendipity, whereas recent AI frameworks are providing more systematic, efficient, and robust strategies for materials design. Whether in molecular design, reaction-condition optimization, or new-materials screening, researchers must search vast chemical and compositional spaces for candidate systems with desired properties, while conventional manual experimentation is unable to systematically explore such enormous spaces [1–3]. Moreover, materials properties are often governed by multiscale coupling from atomic structures and microstructures to macroscopic morphologies. The relationships among molecular structures, synthetic routes, experimental conditions, materials compositions, and final properties are highly nonlinear, making traditional approaches based on empirical experience and single theoretical models prone to long development cycles, high costs, high failure rates, and limited reproducibility [1–3]. Accordingly, artificial intelligence, particularly machine learning and deep learning, is driving chemistry and materials research from experience-driven practice toward a data-driven, algorithm-assisted, and intelligently optimized research paradigm [1–4].
It should be noted that the application of AI in chemistry and materials science did not begin with ChatGPT. Earlier approaches, including expert systems, quantitative structure–activity relationship (QSAR) models, support vector machines, random forests, deep neural networks, graph neural networks, and machine-learning potentials, have already played important roles in molecular property prediction, drug discovery, reaction prediction, materials screening, and high-throughput computation [1,5]. Ramos et al. [5] reviewed that expert systems such as DENDRAL were used to infer molecular structures from mass spectrometry data, QSAR models were developed to predict relationships between chemical structures and activities, and later methods such as neural networks, support vector machines, random forests, and graph neural networks were gradually introduced into cheminformatics, drug discovery, molecular property prediction, and synthesis prediction. Mroz et al. [4] further noted that AI is expanding from accelerating computation and exploring chemical space to experimental planning, data-characterization pipelines, and laboratory automation, thereby exerting a cross-disciplinary impact on chemical research. Therefore, the current generation of AI technologies represented by large language models and agents should be understood as a new stage in the long-term development of AI4Chem and AI4Mat, rather than as a simple replacement for existing domain-specific models and computational methods.
The rapid development of large language models (LLMs) is closely associated with the Transformer architecture. The Transformer proposed by Vaswani et al. [6] is built upon the attention mechanism and overcomes the limitations of traditional recurrent neural networks and convolutional neural networks in sequence modeling, making it a key technical foundation for modern LLMs. After OpenAI released ChatGPT in November 2022, conversational LLMs entered public awareness through natural-language interaction, enabling researchers to perform tasks such as literature summarization, code generation, problem decomposition, preliminary experimental planning, and scientific writing assistance using ordinary language [7]. Pei et al. [8] noted that, since the release of ChatGPT, applications of NLP and LLMs in materials science have attracted rapidly increasing attention, with related studies covering literature mining, knowledge graphs, materials recommendation, automated materials discovery, and sustainable materials design. Unlike conventional machine-learning models, which typically rely on structured data, manual feature engineering, and task-specific modeling, LLMs can process natural language, chemical language, semi-structured experimental records, and scientific literature, thereby providing a new technical basis for research assistance across tools, data sources, and tasks [5,8].
Chemistry and materials science inherently rely on language to record, communicate, and reproduce experimental knowledge. Scientific papers, patents, laboratory records, reaction procedures, materials formulations, and safety protocols contain substantial tacit knowledge in textual form. Accordingly, NLP and LLMs are particularly suitable for extracting structured knowledge from scientific texts, including physical quantities, materials compositions, synthesis conditions, reaction pathways, named entities, performance metrics, and experimental observations [8–10]. Pei et al. [8] pointed out that NLP and language models can extract information from large volumes of text and support materials discovery, knowledge-graph construction, and sustainable materials design. Dagdelen et al. [9] demonstrated that fine-tuned LLMs can extract structured domain knowledge from complex scientific texts, thereby enabling the construction of larger-scale scientific databases. Polak et al. [10] further showed that general-purpose language models, combined with human supervision and prompt engineering, can extract materials data from full-text materials science papers and reduce the programming and domain-expertise barriers associated with data extraction. These studies indicate that the scientific value of LLMs lies not only in text generation, but also in transforming unstructured knowledge into searchable, computable, and reusable data resources.
Meanwhile, generative artificial intelligence is profoundly influencing molecular design and inverse problems in chemistry. Many chemical research tasks are essentially inverse problems, in which molecular structures, materials compositions, or synthetic routes must be identified from desired target properties. Conventional approaches usually rely on repeated cycles of “conception–synthesis/measurement–evaluation”, whereas variational autoencoders, generative adversarial networks, reinforcement learning, Bayesian optimization, diffusion models, and LLM-based molecular generation methods provide new possibilities for accelerating this cycle [11]. Sumita et al. [11] systematically reviewed the development of AI for small-molecule design and noted that molecular generative AI is becoming an important tool for addressing inverse chemical problems, although its practical application remains constrained by training-data quality, synthetic accessibility, experimental validation, and actual performance improvement. Jablonka et al. [12] showed that properly fine-tuned LLMs can be applied to molecular and materials property prediction as well as reaction-yield prediction. Guo et al. [13] evaluated LLMs on eight chemistry-related benchmark tasks and found that LLMs can be competitive in some tasks but still show clear limitations in complex tasks such as reaction prediction. Therefore, the general language ability of LLMs should not be directly equated with reliable chemical reasoning; their chemical applications still require task-level evaluation, experimental validation, and verification using domain-specific tools.
Although LLMs possess powerful natural-language interaction capabilities, relying solely on knowledge encoded in model parameters is insufficient for rigorous scientific research. Because model training data are temporally limited and the generation process may produce hallucinations, incorrect citations, and untraceable reasoning, LLM-based systems for scientific applications must be integrated with external knowledge sources, databases, and domain-specific tools. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) introduces retrieved evidence from external documents or databases into the generation process, thereby partially mitigating outdated knowledge, factual errors, and the lack of source traceability. Lewis et al. [14] proposed the RAG framework, which combines parametric memory with non-parametric external retrieval and generates more specific and factual text in knowledge-intensive natural-language processing tasks. Gao et al. [15] systematically reviewed RAG techniques and highlighted RAG as an important approach for improving the factuality, updatability, and interpretability of LLMs. Pei et al. [8] also noted that, in materials science, RAG and knowledge graphs can enhance the reliability of LLM outputs and reduce the risk of hallucination.
Beyond RAG, tool use and agent technologies are further transforming LLMs from text-generation models into executors of scientific research workflows. Toolformer, proposed by Schick et al. [16], demonstrated that language models can learn when to invoke external APIs, how to pass parameters, and how to incorporate returned results into subsequent generation. The ReAct framework proposed by Yao et al. [17] alternates reasoning traces and action steps, enabling models to access external knowledge bases or environmental information during inference and dynamically update task plans. Wang et al. [18] conceptualized LLM-based autonomous agents as systems composed of perception, memory, planning, action, and tool-use modules, and noted that such agents are expanding from task execution in closed environments to complex scenarios in natural sciences and engineering. At the infrastructure level, interoperability protocols such as the Model Context Protocol (MCP) have begun to standardize connections between AI applications and external data sources or tool systems, providing an emerging engineering foundation for tool orchestration and context management in agent systems [19]. Together, these advances indicate that the integration of LLMs with RAG, tool use, memory mechanisms, and task-planning modules is accelerating the development of agentic workflows, enabling AI systems to progressively coordinate information retrieval, reasoning and analysis, tool invocation, and task execution.
In chemistry, representative LLM-based agent systems have already demonstrated considerable potential for scientific assistance. ChemCrow, proposed by Bran et al. [20], integrates an LLM with 18 expert-designed chemistry tools to construct a chemistry-oriented agent for organic synthesis, drug discovery, and materials design. Coscientist, developed by Boiko et al. [21], further illustrates the potential of LLM systems for autonomous chemical research by demonstrating capabilities in synthesis planning, public-data retrieval, hardware-documentation search, code execution, automated-equipment control, and experimental optimization. These studies suggest that the core value of LLMs lies not merely in answering chemical questions, but in serving as natural-language interfaces and task-planning engines that connect literature, databases, computational models, experimental platforms, and human experts. Ramos et al. [5] also emphasized that when LLMs are integrated with tools for searching synthetic procedures, querying experimental properties, and controlling robotic synthesis and characterization systems, autonomous agents become highly compatible with chemical automation research. Accordingly, the development of chemical agents should not be limited to answer generation, but should instead focus on closed-loop research workflows for real scientific tasks, including retrieval, reasoning, tool use, validation, and report generation.
In materials science, AI is evolving along two parallel trajectories: task-specific AI and generalist AI. Task-specific AI is typically used for property prediction, structural characterization, experimental optimization, and materials screening, whereas generalist AI places greater emphasis on knowledge representation, reasoning and planning, tool orchestration, and human–AI collaboration. Li et al. [2] described AI4Mat as two complementary pathways: task-specific AI functions as a “solver” for specific scientific problems, while generalist AI acts as a “coordinator” that connects knowledge, tools, and workflows; together, these pathways point toward automated materials discovery and self-driving laboratories. This framework suggests that future intelligent systems for chemistry and materials science should not simply replace domain-specific models with a single general model, but should instead integrate domain models, knowledge bases, experimental data, simulation tools, and agentic workflows [2,8]. Pei et al. [8] also emphasized that the potential of language models in materials research lies not only in text processing, but also in their integration with knowledge graphs, RAG, automated experimentation, and sustainable materials design.
Automated experimentation and self-driving laboratories represent important directions in AI for Chemistry and AI for Materials. Self-driving laboratories generally integrate robotic experimental platforms, online characterization systems, data-analysis software, and decision-making algorithms, enabling closed-loop experimental design, execution, real-time analysis, and parameter optimization. Tom et al. [22] noted that self-driving laboratories can significantly accelerate chemistry and materials discovery through experimental-process automation and autonomous experimental planning. Häse et al. [23] discussed the role of next-generation self-driving laboratories in experimental optimization and automated discovery; Burger et al. [24] demonstrated a mobile robotic chemist for autonomous search of photocatalytic systems; Kusne et al. [25] showed Bayesian active learning in closed-loop materials discovery; Raccuglia et al. [26] demonstrated that failed experimental data can also support machine-learning-assisted materials discovery; Granda et al. [27] used machine learning to control an organic-synthesis robot in the search for new reactivity; MacLeod et al. [28] constructed a self-driving laboratory for accelerated thin-film materials discovery; and Shields et al. [29] demonstrated the value of Bayesian optimization in chemical reaction optimization. Together, these studies indicate that the integration of AI with automated experimental platforms is driving chemistry and materials research from offline prediction toward online decision-making and closed-loop validation.
However, the direct application of LLMs, agents, and automated experimentation to real scientific workflows still faces several challenges. First, LLMs may produce hallucinations, incorrect citations, unstable reasoning, and irreproducible outputs, which are particularly risky in chemistry and materials research because erroneous reaction suggestions, materials formulations, or safety judgments may lead to experimental failure or safety hazards [5,8,13,30]. Ji et al. [30] systematically reviewed hallucination in natural-language generation and showed that generative models may produce content that is inconsistent with sources or factually incorrect. Bender et al. [31] also discussed potential risks of large-scale language models from the perspectives of data, bias, and environmental costs. Second, chemical and materials data are highly heterogeneous; experimental conditions, failed experiments, negative results, and process parameters are often incompletely recorded, leading to data bias in model training and validation [1,3,26]. Ramos et al. [5] emphasized that LLM applications in chemistry still face challenges related to data quality, model interpretability, standardized benchmarks, and insufficient experimental validation. Gupta et al. [3] also noted that the further development of materials AI remains constrained by data quality, data availability, model generalizability, and limited extrapolation capability in real discovery scenarios. Therefore, LLM and agent systems for chemistry and materials science must establish traceable data sources, reliable tool-use mechanisms, clear boundaries for human supervision, and multi-level model-evaluation frameworks.
Accordingly, next-generation AI systems for chemistry and materials research should move beyond single question-answering models toward intelligent scientific assistants that are retrievable, verifiable, tool-augmented, process-traceable, and capable of collaboration with human experts. Such systems need to integrate the natural-language interaction capability of LLMs, the literature-tracing ability of RAG, the structured representation of knowledge graphs, the reliable prediction capability of domain-specific computational tools, and the task-planning and execution capability of agents. Compared with traditional machine-learning models, these systems can cover the complete scientific workflow of problem formulation, evidence retrieval, hypothesis generation, tool invocation, result analysis, and report generation. Compared with ordinary chatbots, their core requirements are stronger domain reliability, source traceability, experimental safety, and scientific reproducibility. Therefore, motivated by the aforementioned research demands and emerging development trends, this study builds upon the DeerFlow 2.0 framework [41] to design and implement GVIM 2.0, an intelligent research assistant tailored for chemistry and materials science. The system is centered around domain-specific Skills [42], which serve as reusable capability modules for integrating computational chemistry, materials analysis, machine learning modeling, scientific visualization, literature processing, and other scientific research tools. By leveraging the task understanding and planning capabilities of large language models, GVIM 2.0 enables the automatic decomposition of complex research tasks, intelligent tool orchestration, and workflow execution. Through the construction of end-to-end scientific workflows spanning literature review, data analysis, scientific computation, and research report generation, GVIM 2.0 provides researchers with a reliable, scalable, and highly customizable platform for intelligent scientific research support.
1.1 System Design and Evaluation Rationale
GVIM 2.0 is designed as a workflow-level research assistant rather than a stand-alone question-answering model. A typical run starts from the web front end, where the user provides a scientific task, uploads data files, and requests concrete output artifacts. The agent then decomposes the task, selects domain skills or computational tools, executes scripts when needed, writes intermediate and final files, and returns a traceable report. This design follows the same general direction as recent chemistry and materials agent studies: the value of the agent is assessed through the quality of the scientific workflow and its final objective outputs, not by an ad hoc impression of whether the conversation appears fluent [20-25,32,33].
For this reason, the present evaluation deliberately separates two forms of evidence. First, broad public benchmark tests measure chemistry and materials knowledge, reasoning, and domain-specific problem solving. Second, task-native evaluation workflows evaluate whether GVIM can organize complete scientific workflows that produce measurable outputs such as prediction errors, extraction F1 scores, retrieval top-k accuracy, or active-search enrichment factors. No subjective custom score is used as a primary metric.

We evaluated GVIM on 400 public chemistry and materials questions assembled from ChemBench, MaScQA, and ChemLLMBench [13,34,35]. The suite combines the previously completed 360-question evaluation with an additional 40-question public extension. All questions were submitted through the GVIM workflow, and scoring followed the native answer-key or task-specific rule for each benchmark. To isolate the contribution of the agentic workflow, we compared the full GVIM system with a same-base API-only setting that used the underlying model without the GVIM workflow layer.

4. Retrospective Active Discovery of BACE Inhibitor Candidates
As a more research-like case study, we evaluated whether GVIM can organize a retrospective active-discovery workflow for high-activity beta-site amyloid precursor protein cleaving enzyme 1 (BACE) inhibitor candidates. The public BACE molecular property dataset was treated as a closed candidate library with pIC50 as the activity label [36]. High-activity targets were defined as the top 5% of molecules by pIC50. Starting from 30 randomly queried molecules, each policy selected batches of 10 candidates up to a total budget of 150 queries over 20 random seeds. During each simulated run, model training and candidate selection used only labels that had already been queried; the remaining pIC50 values were used only as the oracle when a molecule was selected and for final metric computation.

7. Temporal External Validation of BACE1 Activity
To test temporal generalization under a stronger label-isolation protocol, we performed a two-stage front-end workflow for human BACE1 (ChEMBL target CHEMBL4822). Stage 1 used 5,296 compounds associated with documents published through 2015 for all feature design and model selection. Measurements from 2016–2017 were excluded as a temporal buffer, and 1,598 non-overlapping compounds associated with documents from 2018 onward formed the external set. Exact molecule overlap was removed by InChIKey; 1,275 external compounds (79.8%) were scaffold-novel relative to training. External pIC50 labels were not provided during Stage 1.
Model selection used three-fold GroupKFold grouped by Murcko scaffold with Morgan fingerprints (radius 2, 1,024 bits) and eight RDKit descriptors. RandomForestRegressor was selected by the lowest mean validation MAE (0.579 ± 0.016), compared with Ridge (0.676) and ExtraTrees (0.628). After predictions were frozen, Stage 2 joined the public external labels by compound_id without retraining. The selected model achieved MAE = 0.822, RMSE = 1.028, and R² = 0.369, outperforming Ridge (MAE = 0.953, R² = 0.105) and the mean baseline (MAE = 1.185, R² = -0.123).
At a 10% screening budget, the model recovered 42 of 161 top-decile external actives (Recall@10% = 0.261; EF10 = 2.61). Known-scaffold compounds gave Recall@10% = 0.424 and EF10 = 4.15, whereas the scaffold-novel subset retained non-random prioritization (Recall@10% = 0.246; EF10 = 2.46) but showed higher prediction error. These findings support retrospective temporal prioritization while also quantifying the loss of extrapolative accuracy for new chemotypes; they do not constitute prospective inhibitor discovery or experimental validation.

8. Discussion
The current evidence supports three bounded claims. First, the full GVIM workflow achieved higher accuracy than the same-base API-only setting on the paired public benchmark. Because no component-level ablation was performed, this difference is attributed to the integrated system rather than to any individual module. Second, the system can initiate and complete heterogeneous chemistry and materials workflows from the front end, including machine learning, data extraction, spectral retrieval, and retrospective fixed-budget prioritization. Third, the use of objective metrics makes the evaluation workflows reproducible and reviewable: each reported number is derived from public labels, public benchmark rules, publisher XML, or established ranked-retrieval protocols.
At the same time, the current manuscript should avoid presenting the system as a chemistry or materials discovery engine with established discovery-level performance. The 400-question benchmark is broad but does not by itself prove autonomous research capability. Some evaluation workflows are intentionally small to control computational cost, and the ChEMU entity extraction result is useful as a workflow evaluation case but not yet competitive with specialized information extraction systems. Stronger publication claims will require frozen prompts, additional agent baselines, component ablations, tool-use accuracy analysis, and a central scientific case that produces a new curated resource, a validated candidate set, or a quantitative structure-property insight.
The temporal BACE1 experiment adds a stricter test of generalization to the workflow evidence. The reduced performance on scaffold-novel compounds is scientifically informative: the agent completed the label-withheld data-to-model-to-evaluation chain and recovered top-decile activity above random, but the quantitative gap shows that workflow completion does not remove the extrapolation limits of fingerprint-based models. This distinction between system-level execution and model-level scientific validity is central to the intended use of GVIM.
9. Methods
9.1 Benchmark construction and scoring
The benchmark suite contained 400 public chemistry and materials questions: 250 ChemBench questions, 97 MaScQA questions, and 53 ChemLLMBench questions. GVIM outputs and API-only outputs were scored with the native answer key or benchmark-specific scoring rule for each task family. Because the three sources use different scoring definitions, the 400-question total is reported as a descriptive aggregate, while per-benchmark results remain the primary interpretable units.
9.2 Task-native workflow scoring
Regression workflows used MAE, RMSE, and R² on held-out labeled data. Extraction workflows used exact or relaxed precision, recall, and F1 after deterministic normalization where appropriate. Retrieval workflows used top-k accuracy and mean reciprocal rank. These metrics are standard task-family metrics and are not custom subjective scores.
9.3 Statistical analysis
For paired benchmark comparison, we used exact McNemar testing on the full-vs-API contingency table and bootstrap confidence intervals for the paired accuracy difference. For retrospective discovery workflows, endpoint differences between surrogate-guided selection policies and random selection were summarized across random seeds and evaluated with bootstrap confidence intervals.
9.4 BACE1 temporal external-validation protocol
Public ChEMBL BACE1 IC50 records were standardized to pIC50 and aggregated by compound. Training compounds were restricted to document years ≤2015; 2016–2017 records were excluded; and external compounds were restricted to document years ≥2018. Exact molecule overlap was removed by InChIKey. Model selection used only the historical training file and three-fold GroupKFold grouped by Bemis–Murcko scaffold. Candidate models were Ridge, RandomForestRegressor and ExtraTreesRegressor using Morgan fingerprints (radius 2, 1,024 bits) plus eight RDKit descriptors. The prespecified selection criterion was mean validation MAE.
The selected model was refit to all historical compounds and generated external_predictions.csv before gold labels were supplied. Stage 2 verified row alignment and the frozen prediction content, joined labels one-to-one by compound_id, and did not retrain or tune a model. MAE, RMSE and R² used their standard definitions. For ranking evaluation, actives were the highest external pIC50 decile and the query set was the highest predicted-pIC50 decile, with deterministic compound_id tie breaking. Recall@10% was the fraction of top-decile actives recovered; EF10 divided the selected-set active fraction by external active prevalence. Metrics were recomputed separately for all, known-scaffold and scaffold-novel compounds.
10. Limitations and Remaining Work
The current data support a strong systems and workflow-evaluation manuscript, but a Chemical Science submission needs a central scientific result beyond platform performance.
The API-only comparison should be expanded to include agent baselines such as original DeerFlow, generic ReAct, GVIM without chemistry/materials skills, and GVIM without verification.
The tool-use layer should be evaluated explicitly with tool-selection accuracy, parameter accuracy, successful execution rate, cost, latency, and failure-mode analysis.
The five evaluation workflows should be frozen as preregistered evaluation prompts with archived input files, outputs, and source data to reduce any perception of cherry-picking.
The strongest next step is a larger scientific case that links literature/data extraction, model building, virtual screening, and independent public-data or computational validation.
